## Code

```python
class Value:

    def __init__(self, data, _children=(), _op='', label=''):
        self.data = data
        self._prev = set(_children)
        self.grad = 0
        self._op = _op
        self.label = label

    def __repr__(self):
        return f"Value(data={self.data})"

    def __add__(self, other):
        return Value(self.data + other.data, (self, other), '+')  # a + b -> a.__add__(b)

    def __mul__(self, other):
        return Value(self.data * other.data, (self, other), '-')


a = Value(2.0, label='a')
b = Value(-3.0, label='b')
c = Value(10.0, label='c')
e = a*b;   e.label = 'e'
d = e + c; d.label = 'd'
f = Value(-2.0, label='f')
L = d*f;   L.label = 'L'
```

## The Definition of the Derivative

$$
\frac{dL}{dd} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{L(d+h) - L(d)}{h}
$$

In plain text: `(f(x+h) - f(x)) / h`.

---

## Chain Rule

> The chain rule states that knowing the instantaneous rate of change of `Z` with respect to `Y`, and `Y` relative to `X`, allows one to calculate the instantaneous rate of change of `Z` relative to `X` as a **product** of those two rates of change.

**Example.** If a car travels twice as fast as a bicycle, and the bicycle is four times as fast as a walking man, then the car travels `2 × 4 = 8` times as fast as the man.

Let `z`, `y`, and `x` be the (variable) positions of the car, the bicycle, and the walking man, respectively. The rate of change of the relative positions of the car and the bicycle is

$$
\frac{dz}{dy} = 2.
$$

Similarly,

$$
\frac{dy}{dx} = 4.
$$

So, the rate of change of the relative positions of the car and the walking man is

$$
\frac{dz}{dx} = \frac{dz}{dy} \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = 2 \cdot 4 = 8.
$$

The rate of change of positions is the ratio of the speeds, and the speed is the derivative of the position with respect to time; that is,

$$
\frac{dz}{dx} = \frac{\dfrac{dz}{dt}}{\dfrac{dx}{dt}},
$$

or, equivalently,

$$
\frac{dz}{dt} = \frac{dz}{dx} \cdot \frac{dx}{dt},
$$

which is also an application of the chain rule.



$$
\frac{dL}{dc} = \frac{dL}{dd} \cdot \frac{dd}{dc}
$$

---

## Backpropagation

> Backpropagation is just a recursive application of the chain rule backwards through the computation graph.

## Questions

### 1. Why does `(f(x+h) - f(x)) / h` approximate the derivative? What goes wrong if `h` is too small or too large?

It comes from the definition `lim h→0 (f(x+h)-f(x))/h` — we pick a finite small `h` and use the **secant slope** to approximate the **tangent slope**.

- **`h` too large**: the secant deviates from the tangent; higher-order Taylor terms `O(h)` are no longer negligible, giving large **truncation error**.
- **`h` too small**: `f(x+h)` and `f(x)` are nearly equal in floating point, and the subtraction suffers **catastrophic cancellation** — significant digits are lost (roundoff error blows up).
- Empirically, the best `h` for float64 is about `√ε ≈ 1e-8`. But this only matters for **numerical** differentiation; autograd uses the **analytical** chain rule and avoids the issue entirely.

### 2. What does a `Value` object store besides `.data`? Why is `._prev` needed?

It also stores `_prev` (set of predecessor nodes), `_op` (the operator that produced it), `grad` (gradient, initialized to 0), and `label` (a debug name).

- `_prev` does **not** store leaf nodes. It stores the **two operands that produced the current node** — i.e. the **parent nodes** in the computation graph (Karpathy calls them `children` because he thinks of it tree-wise).
- Its purpose is to keep the computation explicitly as a **DAG** (directed acyclic graph). During backprop, we start from the loss and **traverse backward along `_prev`** through the whole graph to propagate gradients layer by layer — this is **where autograd's "graph" actually lives**.

### 3. How are `a + b` and `a * b` overloaded to return `Value` objects?

Via Python's **dunder methods**: `__add__` and `__mul__`. When you write `a + b`, Python calls `a.__add__(b)`.

- The return value is not a plain number but a **new `Value` object**, which also records:
  - `data = self.data + other.data` (the numerical result)
  - `_children = (self, other)` (pointers back to the two operands — needed to build the graph)
  - `_op = '+'` (the operator — backprop dispatches different derivative rules based on this)
- **Key point:** overloading isn't just computing the value — it simultaneously builds the graph. Without this, there's nothing for backprop to walk.

### 4. Why does Karpathy emphasize "scalar-valued" — why start from scalars instead of tensors?

A scalar is just a special case of a tensor (0-dimensional), 

If you jump straight to tensors in NumPy or PyTorch, your head fills up with **linear-algebra bookkeeping** — matrix transposes, broadcasting, dimension matching. The problem: all of that **obscures the core logic of autograd (backpropagation): the chain rule**.

With scalar operations (e.g. $L = a \times b + c$), the derivative is just plain numerical multiplication. Scalars let you clearly observe two things:

- **Local derivative**: how each operation node converts an increment in its input into an increment in its output.
- **Gradient propagation**: how the chain rule, like a relay race, passes the gradient from the output end back to the input end.


- Starting with scalars lets you see the **essence** without being distracted by engineering details like shapes, broadcasting, Jacobians, or matrix transposes.
- The tensor version is essentially the scalar version "batched" and "vectorized" — PyTorch's internal graph structure is identical to micrograd's.
- **Pedagogical order:** first understand how one scalar backprops → then how a batch of scalars backprops in parallel → finally real PyTorch tensor autograd. Starting with tensors tends to **conflate autograd with linear algebra**, making both harder to learn.
